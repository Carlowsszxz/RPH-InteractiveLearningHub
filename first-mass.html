Where Did the First Mass Take Place?

Historical Context: Beyond the "Firsts"

The obsession with identifying historical "firsts" often leads to oversimplification of complex events. However, this case study transcends mere trivia. Rather than focusing on the symbolic prestige of the location, we examine the First Catholic Mass as a historiographical exercise—demonstrating how historians use evidence, compare sources, and apply critical interpretation in reconstructing the past. The question of where the Mass occurred reveals the evolving nature of historical scholarship and the importance of rigorous source analysis.

The Three-Century Butuan Claim

For more than three centuries, Butuan was accepted as the site of the first Catholic Mass in the Philippines. This widespread belief culminated in the erection of a monument in 1872 near the Agusan River, commemorating the arrival of Magellan's expedition and the celebration of Mass on April 8, 1521. The Butuan claim was based largely on an elementary reading of primary sources that was accepted and repeated in historical narratives.

However, as historical research methods improved toward the end of the nineteenth century and the beginning of the twentieth century, scholars began to reexamine these sources more critically. This reassessment challenged the long-accepted interpretation and opened a significant debate between Butuan and Limasawa as the possible site of the first Mass.

Primary Sources: The Foundation of the Debate

Historians rely on only two major primary sources to determine the site of the first Catholic Mass in the Philippines:

Source 1: Francisco Albo's Log — A pilot who survived the circumnavigation and recorded navigational details, distances, and latitudes.
Source 2: Antonio Pigafetta's Account — The most detailed narrative, "Primo viaggio intorno al mondo" (First Voyage Around the World), providing eyewitness testimony of events and activities during the voyage.

Both sources were written by members of Magellan's expedition who witnessed the events firsthand. Analyzing these accounts provides crucial insights into where the Mass actually occurred.

Albo's Geographical Evidence

Francisco Albo's log describes the expedition's movement from the Ladrones (Marianas) to Suluan, then to Homonhon, and later to an island called Mazava, located at approximately 9°40' North latitude. In Mazava, Albo noted the planting of a cross on a mountain-top from which three islands to the west and southwest could be seen.

Critically, Albo's geographical descriptions strongly correspond to the location of Limasawa, situated at the southern tip of Leyte. His account does not align with Butuan's riverine geography, as no river was mentioned in relation to Mazava—a striking omission for a site as geographically significant as Butuan.

📜 EYEWITNESS ACCOUNT 1

📄 Pigafetta on the Route and Arrival at Mazaua

Antonio Pigafetta provided the most detailed narrative of the expedition's movements. He documented that the expedition traveled from Homonhon (reached on March 17, 1521) westward toward Leyte, then southward, and finally to an island called Mazaua on March 28, 1521 (Holy Thursday).

"It is twenty-five leagues from the Acquada, and is called Mazaua. It lies at a latitude of nine and two-thirds degrees towards the Arctic Pole (i.e., North)."

— Antonio Pigafetta

Pigafetta's geographical descriptions—particularly the island setting, coastal features, and proximity to Leyte and Cebu—corroborate Albo's log and further support the identification of Mazaua as Limasawa.

📜 EYEWITNESS ACCOUNT 2

📄 Pigafetta's Account: Easter Sunday and the First Mass

Pigafetta provided crucial details about the celebration of the Mass. On Sunday, March 31, 1521 (Easter Sunday), Magellan sent a priest ashore in the early morning to prepare for the celebration. Later that day:

"Early in the morning of Sunday, the last of March and Easter day, Magellan sent the priest ashore with some men to prepare for the Mass. Later in the morning Magellan landed with some fifty men and Mass was celebrated, after which a cross was venerated. In the afternoon they returned ashore to plant the cross on the summit of the highest hill. In attendance both at the Mass and at the planting of the cross were the king of Mazaua and the king of Butuan."

— Antonio Pigafetta

This detailed description firmly situates the celebration of the Mass on an island, further weakening the claim that it occurred in Butuan, which was a settlement located along a river.

Critical Analysis: The River Question

Key Evidence: The absence of any mention of a river in Pigafetta's account of Mazaua undermines the Butuan claim. Butuan was a riverine settlement along the Agusan River—a geographical feature too significant to overlook.

Jesuit historian Miguel A. Bernad, in his work "Butuan or Limasawa? The Site of the First Mass in the Philippines: A Reexamination of Evidence" (1981), emphasized a critical omission in Pigafetta's account that undermines the Butuan hypothesis. Pigafetta made no reference to a river in describing Mazaua or the activities there. This silence is particularly significant because:

  • Butuan's defining geographical feature was the Agusan River, where it was situated
  • A riverine environment would have been difficult for an eyewitness to overlook
  • Pigafetta later described a river journey to Mindanao after Magellan's death, demonstrating that he was attentive to riverine environments when present

This contrast strongly supports the conclusion that Mazaua was not Butuan but Limasawa.

Conclusion: Methodology Over Tradition

Central Insight: The question of where the first Catholic Mass occurred demonstrates that history is not fixed by tradition but refined through disciplined analysis of evidence.

While Butuan was long accepted as the site due to tradition and commemoration, careful reexamination of primary sources—particularly the accounts of Francisco Albo and Antonio Pigafetta—demonstrates that Limasawa more accurately fits the geographical and narrative evidence. The shift from Butuan to Limasawa as the scholarly consensus illustrates the evolving nature of historical knowledge.

More importantly, this case study shows that history is not merely about celebrating "firsts" or identifying locations. Instead, it is about understanding how historical knowledge is constructed, challenged, and refined through disciplined analysis of evidence. The First Catholic Mass in the Philippines remains a powerful moment in Philippine history—and examining where it took place teaches us how to be better historians.
